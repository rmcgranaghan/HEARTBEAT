Introduction to Aurora Borealis Data​​​​​​​

Predicting Auroral phenomenon is vital for understanding the impact of solar storms and knowing when high levels electron and ion particle precipitations will impact (damage) satellites and where in the paorbits the impact is observed.

 

We are applying cutting-edge techniques from the field of ML to one of the most important challenges in space weather (Magnetosphere-Ionosphere Coupling; Particle Precipitation), particularly the total electron flux as observed by the 
Defense Meteorological Satellite Program (DMSP) satellites. These fluxes represent electrons that come from the magnetosphere and move along magnetic field lines into the upper atmosphere (100-1000 km altitude). When they impact molecules (e.g., our atmosphere) they cause ionizations and visible emissions -- the aurora

 

Auroral particle precipitation is a critical input to global circulation models, which describes the effects that the energy deposited by electrons and ions has - causing ionizations that change the ionosphere and affect radio communications, heating the thermosphere leading to enhanced neutral density.  This changes the orbits of all objects within the upper atmosphere and the coupling to the entire Earth system

 

Our models are based on data from the Defense Meteorology Satellite Program spacecraft, using data beginning in 1985. These spacecraft carry a particle detector (SSJ) which is sensitive to particles with characteristic energies between 30 eV and 30 keV.  As an illustrative example, the path for one satellite relative to the surface of the earth's surface is shown below. Note that this is just one day and the orbits evolve as time goes one.  Also all of the satellites have different orbits.

Figure: One day for one Satellite 10/10/2010 using subset of samples (one every minute). Data is only available (and relevant) for latitudes greater than 25 degrees, our models focus on the greater than 45 degrees region.

 

Note that looking at the paths of the satellites along the earth is misleading, because the aurora is modeled in a different reference frame than that which the earth rotates through. Therefore the Magnetic local time rather than the longitude is used for the Aurora. This reference frame is always facing the sun in the same direction. A subset of this data is shown in the figure below and a zoomed in view of the relevant spatial domain for our modeling purposes is shown in the following figure.

Figure: Same data as previous plot but viewed from the magnetic north pole. The 24 hour Local time that doesn't rotate is shown with values from 0 to 24 hours, similar to timezones. We assume conjugacy and map the southern hemisphere observations to the northern hemisphere when training and testing our models.

Figure: Zoomed in view of previous figure.  This is the view that is used for the results shown in boxes 3 and 4.  There is insignificant auroral activity below 45 degrees latitude, so only the upper latitudes are modeled.

 

We combine the measures from multiple satellites from multiple years to get an almost full spatial coverage for the greater than 45 degrees magnetic latitudes. The below figure shows an example of how this is done.  Note that the spatial domain is Magnetic local time 0:00 to 24:00 and latitude 45 to 90 degrees.  

Figure: Combing multiple obervations over multiple years from multiple satellites to create a dataset that nealy covers all local time.  McGranaghan et al., [2015]

 

Solar Wind Parameters and Space Weather Indices:

Our models utilize the time dependent NASA OMNIWeb space weather data as inputs.  The solar wind parameters and magnetic field values are primary examples. All of the details of our dataset and python scripts for data preparation are shown here in our GIT repo:

 https://github.com/rmcgranaghan/precipNet

 

Deep Learning for Electron Energy Flux Prediction

Not a Typical Multidimensional Time Series ML Problem!
  • Inputs are time series and the target to be predicted represents both spatial (2D) and temporal variations
  • For any time step, target measurement (validations a for the predictions) only exist at one or a few spatial locations

Inputs: Non-spatial data (Indices) at 5 min cadence

Target Output: Total electron energy flux at any given location (nowcast)

 

-> Non-time series validation targets 

  • Validation samples: Sparse in time and spatial dimensions

---> ML problem is solved as regression problem not time series

  • Even though "time series" of input data values are histories.

A bit of nomenclature:  The Target (total electron energy flux) is the output of model which can be used for a nowcast but is also used to train, test, and validate a machine learning model (our deep neural network). The predicted targets are compared against train and test targets during model training, and then once training is completed, the predicted target is compared to the validation targets.  

Our withheld validation set consists of all DMSP F16 satellite data in the year 2010, more than 50000 data points. Taking a consecutive year of data for validation, rather than randomly sampling the full database, prevents influence by autocorrelations in the data

 

Challenges for model to overcome:

Are variations in observed training target data temporally or spatially dependent or both? Model needs to deconvolve temporal and spatial responses as there is only one timestep for each 2D sample's position in the orbit.

 

Model Inputs: 148 values total
[
Spatial and temporal inputs:

Magnetic Latitude, Magnetic local time (transformed to be continuous across 0 to 24 hours and note this is a spatial value), UTC time of day and year (also transformed to be continuous)), and spacecraft id

"Global" time history Inputs:

Instantaneous values: 0, 5, 10, and 15 min ago

Averaged values: 30 and 45 minutes ago and 1, 3, 5, and 6 hour moving averages

Total Electron Energy Flux, representing the particle precipitation

 

Even though we trained most models with all of the 148 inputs, our best model (described more in box 4), uses a 73 input subset of this. By "Global" inputs we mean that they represent a parameter for the whole Earth and do not have a particular spatial location, just a temporal variation.

 

Extensibility to other Space weather modeling problems:

Though we present results for the total electron energy flux, this approach could be used for any critical high-latitude space weather variable (e.g., ionospheric convection, field-aligned currents.)

 

Data Preparation Challenges

Data comes from multiple satellites:

Satellites from "multiple generations of sensors" from 1987 to 2018. 150 total "satellite years"

Magnetic north and south polar regions not adequately represented::

Most of the sample points in the "polar" region are just outside or the auroral region and there is limited coverage of satellites near the center of the poles, this could be skewing the learned spatial representation with uneven sampling.

Figure: DMSP Satellite F16 coverage during 2010 (note that this reference frame rotates with the earth, the previous figure shows local time and this figure shows longitude)

 

Need for Target Transformation

Getting the deep learning model to learn a useful representation is difficult without first transforming the flux to a log scale as it varies by factor of 10^6

Figure: Actual scale of the total electron energy fluxes. Showing the measured values in blue and the predictions in orange.

Figure: Log10 scale of the total electron energy fluxes. Showing the measured values in blue and the predictions in orange. Data has been transformed to a log10 eV/cm^2/ster/s scale.

 

The deep learning models are capable of learning a predictive model when traning with untransformed data, however, thus far our non-transformed models only learn to predict values above the mean and only capture right most mode of our predominately bi-modal distribution of electron fluxes.  In order for a non-log scaled input model to work at all the logarithmic version of the mean square loss function rather than the standard MSE is required.

 

Large range:

  • target varies by factor of 10^6
  • transforming allows training to converge but weights large changes less

---> target transformed to log scale

---> observations that represent unrealistically large fluxes, deemed to be those exceeding the 99.995th percentile (> 7.37 × 1013 eV/cm2/sr/s) are removed as being deemed sensor anomalies.

 

Unbalanced data:

Small amount of storms, large “tail” in target, histogram shows small number of high flux samples.

Figure: Orange is the true value and blue is the prediction. X-axis units are eV/cm^2/ster/s/. Y-axis is number of occurances for each histogram bin.

 

Multimodel, spatially and temporally:

Target points composed of multiple "modes" of Gaussian distributions. Multiple clusters of similar sample flux points observed when viewing histogram of the logarithm of the target flux.

Figure: Orange is the true value and blue is the prediction. Data has been transformed to a log10 eV/cm^2/ster/s scale.

Inputs shown: The AE, AL, and AU indices describe the disturbance level recorded by auroral zone magnetometers. The interplanetary magnetic field is a vector quantity with a three axis component, two of which (Bx and By) are orientated parallel to the ecliptic. The third component, the Bz value is perpendicular to the ecliptic and is created by waves and other disturbances in the solar wind.

This movie shows how the predicted flux increases the tauroral electrojet indices increase during a space weather storm.  A peak value of ~12 (log10 of the flux which has dimensions of eV/cm^2/ster/second) and is observed in the time evolving spatial plots as a very bright yellow section in the auroral region.  Note that the right model with our special "tail loss" loss function does a significantly better job at capturing the peaks.  This best model is described more at the end of the next box of this poster.

OVATION Pyme Github

OVATION relies on bins in latitude and local time, separately fitting a linear model in each bin. Therefore, the model produces discontinuous outputs across bins in a 2D map, while the ML approach, by design, produces continous output for any input latitude and local time.  As the ML model improves, the small scale phenomenon will be represented without having the need to keep adding more bins to the fits.

Auroal Boundary and Region Prediction Models: Adding more diverse model inputs

 

The 2010-2014 subset of the DMSP data offers estimates of the auroral boundary transition regions based on spatial gradients of the electron flux. We used this data to create a classification model of the auroral regions.  The results of this region estimation model are fed into the regression model for electron flux as an additional input and allow us to observe how the model performs in what it thinks is the auroral and polar regions.

Black: Equatorial Region (no activity)

Red: Auroral region

Yellow: Polar region

 

Results:

Using the Auroral region classifier (90% accurate classification), as one experiment, three separate models were trained on each region. In classification 2D color plot the red region is the aurora and the yellow region is the polar region.  

The results show that the mean square error (MSE) for the polar region is the largest and the Equatorial the lower.  Showing that the model is most accurate at predicting the black, no-activity region.  Surprisingly, the Auroral region is only ~20% higher in error (with log10 flux), and the polar region error is ~50% higher.  This means that the model is more accurate at representing the flux near the outer auroral boundary than the inner.  This could also mean that it is possible that there is not enough input information to describe the polar regions flux.

 

Multi-task with auroral boundaries (classification and regression in one neural network)

We developed a model that predicts the auroral region type and the total electron energy flux simultaneously with a custom less function that has a categorical cross entropy part and a part which predicts the residual of the flux from the modal averages of each of the auroral types.

This model actually has 2 output branches, one side with 3 softmax values for predicting the class, and the other side which predicts the flux for each region.  Note that in the loss function, only the model's predicted region value (one of the three regression values) is used in the MSE contribution of the loss.

Loss Function Engineering:

The peak fluxes fall in a long tail of the distribution and one of the outstanding questions in space weather is to capture these tails. This has been a continual focus of our research and the right bimodal region (which is mainly the auroral region) is tested one its own for each model as well as the 99th% percentile for the highest flux values as these are the ones that are the most import to predict in terms of the application of space weather.

 

Our "storm specific" function uses a tail "weighted" loss function:

Loss = (y_true-y_pred)^2 + [ a*(y_true-y_pred)^2 ] * [(y_true> y_r) and (y_pred < y_r)]

 

A special logic basic loss function was found to increase the accuracy in the tail region without significantly decreasing the accuracy in the lower flux region.  This function is a sum of the typical mean square error (MSE) and a special term which is only > 0 in particular circumstances.  

This additional term has the effect of penalizing the neural network when a true high value is predicted below a predetermined threshold.  If the true value is also below this threshold or if both the true and predicted value are over the threshold, then there is no penalty.

For the second term a value for y_r is chosen, where y is the target and r signifies the "right side" of the tail. This value is within the tail region on the right side of the histogram.  When the true value of the flux is greater than this value and the predicted value is less than this value, an additional loss penalty is added to the MSE, one which is proportional by the factor "a" to the MSE, (y_true-y_pred)^2.  

 

 

Figure: Actual scale histogram. Showing larger numbers of high flux predictions for the model with the new loss function.

Figure: Log10 scale histogram

 

This is last figure, it is clearly shown that by adding this loss function term the validation errors are significally decreased for flux values greater than 11 (log10 eV/cm^2/ster/s).  Also not the the threshold based nature of this loss function preserves the accuracy in the other "non-tail" regions, insignificant changing the accuracy there.

Directly using the model for forecasting needs further considerations if not using another model to predict the future input parameters.

When training  for future rather than nowcast values, because we are solving a regression rather than a time-series formulated problem, a timestep (for example 1 hour) must be preselected for the matching points. The inputs to the model are then available at all past timesteps (5 minute resolution for some indices and 1 hour resolution for others), and therefore for each sample can have matching indices for 1 hour back. 


Because for a fixed position (latitude and longitude) there are no consistent temporally repeating points, one does not, for example, have training data for multiple future predictions for each position. 


If this was a typical time series problem, one would pick a latitude and longitude and have the ML model then predict the target flux at multiple future timesteps, for example: 
(lat, long, present indice 1, present indice 2, ...) ---> Flux at time 1, time 2, time 3 for input lat and long
 
However this is not possible because for each training sample, each timestep only has one flux for one position, or equivalently for each unique position there is only one flux for one timestep.

Abstract

We have developed multiple Deep Learning models to predict the global auroral particle precipitation, including a novel multi-task model that uses solar wind, geomagnetic indices, and their time histories as input to predict the total electron energy flux, total number flux, and auroral boundaries globally. We show results for nowcasts (prediction at the time of observation) and forecasts from one up to several hours. More than 40 satellite years of Defense Meteorological Satellite Program (DMSP) precipitation data provide the observations, which are split into training and validation sets.. DMSP data provide local information along the orbital paths of the spacecraft, and require intensive data treatment. We detail the data preparation process as well as the model development that will be illustrative for many similar time series global regression problems in space weather. We highlight a central result of importance is our design of a new multi-task deep learning network that simultaneously predicts the auroral region and the precipitation. Details of the influence of model structure are also highlighted, convolutional vs dense layers for example, as well dedicated designs for the multi-modal nature of the rare and extreme space storms.
